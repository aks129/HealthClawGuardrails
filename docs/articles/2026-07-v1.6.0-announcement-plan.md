>
> New in this release:
>
> 🧪 **Lab interpreter** (`Observation/$interpret`) — flags results low/normal/high/critical with HL7 standard codes, plus a plain-language summary written for the *consumer*, not the chart ("worth discussing with your clinician" — never a diagnosis). ## Draft B — Substack outline

**Title:** *An AI found a clinically dangerous bug in the code an AI wrote*
**Subtitle:** What shipping a lab interpreter for health data taught me about review discipline, honest software, and why guardrails have to live server-side.

1. **Cold open** — the bug: glucose 54 mg/dL (nearly critical-low) + a reference range quoted in mmol/L = flagged "HIGH". Two units, one inversion, zero crash. The kind of bug that passes every happy-path test.
2. **The setup** — what HealthClaw Guardrails is (5 properties, between any agent and any FHIR server), why a lab interpreter at all (consumers get lab PDFs they can't read; the goal is outcomes, not labels).
3. **The build** — spec with four principles (consumer > patient, outcomes > labels, accuracy gates inclusion, cite every range or the tests fail). TDD, then *adversarial review as a separate step with a separate agent told to break it*.
4. **The catch** — walk the bug. Why "resource range wins" is right *and* dangerous without unit validation. The fix: trust the lab's range only when units agree; otherwise fall back or say *indeterminate*. "Indeterminate beats guessed" as a design stance.
5. **The bigger point** — health AI safety isn't a model property, it's a *system* property: server-side guardrails, review gates, honest labels ("decision support, not diagnosis"; "calculator, not certified eCQM"), provenance enforced by CI.
6. **Close** — it's MIT, it's on GitHub, the ranges await clinician review (that's in the docs, on purpose). Invitation: clinicians to review the table, engineers to take a `good first issue`.

**Length:** 1,200–1,600 words. **CTA:** star/watch the repo, subscribe for the next build log.
